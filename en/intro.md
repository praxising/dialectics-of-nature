# Introduction

**Frederick Engels**
Written: 1873–1886  
First Published: 1925 (Russian/German edition)

---

Modern natural science has inherited from the past a particular mode of thought – the metaphysical method – which fundamentally hindered the correct understanding of nature.（1）For the metaphysician, things and their mental images, concepts, are fixed, rigid, given objects of investigation, to be considered one after the other and independently of each other.

But when we consider nature, or the history of mankind, or our own intellectual activity, the first picture presented to us is that of an endless web of interconnections and interactions, [1] in which nothing remains what, where, or as it was. This is the dialectical mode of thought, which alone can do justice to the totality of nature's phenomena.

18th-century natural science carried the mechanical conception of nature to its extreme, explaining all changes in nature as merely quantitative changes of pre-existing mechanical motion.（2）But qualitative changes – the transformation of one form of motion into another – cannot be reduced to mere mechanics.

Heat, light, electricity, magnetism – these are not forms of motion of ponderable matter, but forms of motion of matter in general. [2] To understand them, we must go beyond the mechanical standpoint and recognise the qualitative diversity of motion.

In his essay *The Part Played by Labour in the Transition from Ape to Man*, Engels emphasised the decisive role of labour in human origins.（3）This thesis has received new support from contemporary anthropology – but has also been given more complex socio-biological interpretations.


## Commentary

### Limitations of Engels（Historical Context and Questionable Points）

**（1） Semantic Difference of "Metaphysics"**  
Engels' understanding of "metaphysics" here refers primarily to the mechanical view of nature of the 18th century. Today, the term "metaphysics" carries a completely different meaning in the analytic philosophical tradition – referring to the study of being and reality as such. Contemporary readers need to be aware of this historical semantic shift. —— Wang Bo, Department of Philosophy, Peking University, June 2026

**（2） Overgeneralisation of "Mechanics"**  
Engels treats "mechanics" as representative of the mechanical worldview. But in contemporary physics, "mechanics" has developed into a rich discipline encompassing quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics, and other branches. The opposition he draws between "mechanics" and "qualitative diversity" requires more nuanced delineation today. —— Zhou Ming, Institute of Theoretical Physics, June 2026

**（3） Monocausal Theory of Human Origins**  
Engels emphasises the decisive role of labour, but contemporary anthropology views human origins as the result of multiple factors working in concert – climate change, genetic mutation, cultural evolution, and others. Treating labour as the "dominant" factor may underestimate the role of environmental pressures and biological contingency. —— Zhao Min, Department of Anthropology and Genetics, June 2026


### Contemporary Scientific Support（21st-Century Confirmation and Deepening）

**[1] The Holistic View of Ecology**  
Contemporary ecology has revealed the countless interconnecting chains within the biosphere – a local change in any one part can trigger fluctuations across the whole. Holistic thinking has become the fundamental methodology of modern environmental science. —— Wu Lin, Institute of Ecology and Environmental Studies, June 2026

**[2] The Quest for Unified Field Theory**  
The electroweak unification has already achieved the unity of electromagnetic and weak forces, while the unification of gravity with quantum mechanics remains a core challenge for contemporary theoretical physics. The "unity of qualitative diversity" that Engels called for is continuing to unfold in new forms. —— Chen Si, Center for History of Science, June 2026
